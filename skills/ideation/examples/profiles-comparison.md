# Example: One idea, six lenses — what the profiles actually change

| | |
|---|---|
| **Profiles used** | all six: `general`, `startup`, `product-roadmap`, `tech-architecture`, `content-strategy`, `personal-decisions` |
| **Real or illustrative** | Real session |
| **What this shows** | Proof that profiles aren't cosmetic. The same idea, run with different profiles active, produces genuinely different Phase 1s — different reframing, different questions, different frameworks, different make-or-break flags. |

> **TL;DR:** A profile isn't a coat of paint on the same answer. Give the *same* freelancer-finance idea to the `startup` profile and it hunts for a beachhead and an unfair advantage; give it to `tech-architecture` and it restates the thing as a multi-tenant money-and-tax system with a compliance surface; give it to `personal-decisions` and it refuses to collapse "is it worth it" and "should I quit" into one question. Below are the six Phase-1 fingerprints, side by side.

---

## The test

To keep it fair, each profile gets the **same** prompt and we compare only the **first Phase-1 reply** (before any answers diverge the conversation), in a fresh session each time.

- **Venture prompt** (run through `general`, `startup`, `product-roadmap`, `tech-architecture` — all four legitimately apply to a buildable product): *"I want to build an app that helps freelancers manage invoices and taxes."*
- **Creator prompt** (`content-strategy`): *"I want to start a TikTok channel teaching freelancers about money."*
- **Life-decision prompt** (`personal-decisions`): *"Should I quit my agency job to go full-time on my freelancer-tax app?"*

---

## The six fingerprints

| Profile | Reframes the goal as… | First questions probe | Frameworks / lens it reaches for | Flags as make-or-break |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **general** | an idea to understand, broadly | outcome, who + country, constraints | none domain-specific; deliberately brief | "why yours" in a crowded space |
| **startup** | a product that must find a market | beachhead (who + country), unfair advantage, runway, the wedge | beachhead, "why you", willingness-to-pay; names real competitors | a narrow beachhead + a real advantage |
| **product-roadmap** | a roadmap to sequence | stage/team, North Star metric, segment, capacity per quarter, the wedge | North Star, capacity ceiling, beachhead | whether a product/team even exists yet |
| **tech-architecture** | a multi-tenant money-and-tax **system** | functional boundary (move money? file taxes?), scale/NFRs, team skills, jurisdiction | requirements-first, PCI/compliance, Conway's Law, "boring tech", reversibility | the functional boundary + regulated surface |
| **content-strategy** | a **channel**, not a topic | outcome/revenue, hours/week for 90 days, on-camera comfort, credibility, audience, starting point | production sustainability, niche, authority, platform finance-disclosure rules | a production model that survives past month 3 |
| **personal-decisions** | a reversibility-weighted life choice | 10-year regret, the app's real traction, financial buffer, who's affected, time pressure, tested vs assumed constraints | one-way/two-way door, regret minimization, separating the two decisions | whether it's truly "quit now" vs. "go part-time" |

---

## What each profile actually did (condensed)

- **`startup`** — went straight at beachhead and the unfair advantage: *"'freelancers' is far too broad to build for… Pick a real beachhead,"* and *"The unfair advantage — why you? … I'll push hard if the answer is hand-wavy."* Named the crowded field (QuickBooks SE, FreshBooks, Wave, Bonsai, Found).

- **`product-roadmap`** — diagnosed its own mismatch first: *"this profile assumes an existing product with a team… Your phrasing sounds pre-launch, which is really startup territory."* Then anchored on a **North Star metric** and a **capacity ceiling** — *"a roadmap without a North Star metric and a capacity ceiling is just a wishlist."*

- **`tech-architecture`** — restated the idea as a *system*: *"a multi-tenant application…"*, and led with the boundary that dominates everything: *"do you handle the money movement … or just generate invoice PDFs? And on taxes — calculator/reminders only, or actual filing?"* Invoked Conway's Law and "choose boring technology."

- **`general`** — deliberately restrained: *"keeps me from over-front-loading questions,"* just three gaps (outcome, who, constraints). The domain-agnostic baseline the others diverge from.

- **`content-strategy`** — reframed *topic → channel*: *"the idea as stated is a topic, not yet a channel,"* and named the genre's #1 killer up front: *"the single most common way channels like this die is the production model being unsustainable past month three."* Also correctly applied **cross-project isolation** — it explicitly refused to import context from the invoice-app runs.

- **`personal-decisions`** — flipped to autonomy: *"this is the one profile where I lean toward respecting your autonomy over pushing,"* and caught the hidden structure: *"this may be two decisions wearing one coat… 'Is the app worth pursuing full-time?' and 'Should I quit now?' are separable."* Treated it as a high-reversibility-cost, one-way-ish door.

---

## Takeaway

Run the same sentence six ways and you get six genuinely different first moves — not six rewordings of one answer. The profile changes *what the coordinator treats as the real question*: a market to win, a roadmap to sequence, a system to bound, a channel to sustain, or a life decision to slow down on.

Two details worth noticing, because they show the profiles aren't acting blind:
- `product-roadmap` recognized it was the *wrong* tool for a pre-launch idea and offered to switch — a profile that knows its own scope.
- `content-strategy` honored cross-project isolation, refusing to drag the invoice-app threads into a fresh topic.

That's the bar a "profile" has to clear to be more than a label. These cleared it.
