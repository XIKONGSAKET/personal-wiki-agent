# Personal Project Profile

For software, tools, apps, games, scripts, or any build someone is making **for themselves** — not a product, not a business. Solo or tiny scope; success is personal utility, not money or users.

## When to use

- "I want to build X for myself / for my own use"
- A hobby build, a learning project, an internal tool, a personal automation
- A game or app you're making because you want it to exist, not to sell
- Anything where the only "user" is you (or a few friends)

If money, customers, or an audience are the point, use `startup`. If it's a life choice rather than a build, use `personal-decisions`.

## Mindset shift (read this first)

The default brainstorm instinct is to think like a product manager — adoption, ROI, market, competitors, success metrics. **None of that applies here.** There is no market and no customer. The real questions are: will you actually finish and use it? Is it worth your time versus a simpler approach or an existing tool? Are you over-building? Keep the rigor — aim it at *personal utility and wasted effort*, never business viability.

## Flow shape

- **Phase 1 — Understanding:** `full`
- **Phase 2 — Context:** `light` — "what existing tools/libraries would you otherwise use? is it worth building vs. reusing?" Not competitor/market research.
- **Phase 3 — Generation:** `full`
- **Phase 4 — Deep Dive:** `full`
- **Phase 5 — Critique:** `full`, through the **personal lens** (`core/agents/critic.md` → Calibrate to nature): failure = you abandon it / over-built / doesn't save time / simpler would've done. No market/ROI/adoption framing.
- **Phase 6 — Plan:** `light` — the smallest version you'll actually use first; concrete next steps, not a business plan.
- **Scaffold:** available (you may well build it in Claude Code).

## Phase customizations

### Phase 1 — Understanding
- What do you want this to do for you, concretely?
- How will you know it's good enough — what does "I'd actually use this" look like?
- Hard constraints: your time, your skills, tools you already have.
- What's the simplest version that would still be worth it?

### Phase 2 — Context
- What do you currently do instead (by hand, or with another tool)?
- Is there an existing tool/library/template that gets you ~80% there?
- Build-vs-reuse: what's the honest case for building your own?

### Phase 3 — Generation
Variant axes: effort (weekend vs. long build), scope (one feature vs. many), reuse (glue existing tools vs. from scratch), polish (rough-but-works vs. nice).

### Phase 4 — Deep Dive
- What it does, for you
- Core mechanic / main flow
- Components and the tools/stack
- The first thing you'd build and use

### Phase 5 — Critique
Critic through the personal lens. Hardest question: will you actually use it, or is *building* it the fun part and it'll rot after a weekend?

### Phase 6 — Plan
Lightweight: the smallest usable version, the first 2–3 concrete steps, and a "good enough to stop" line so you don't gold-plate.

## Frameworks the coordinator may invoke

- Build-vs-buy / build-vs-reuse
- MVP / smallest-useful-version
- Premortem (personal: will I abandon it?)
- Inversion (what guarantees I never finish or use it)
- YAGNI / scope discipline

## Anti-patterns specific to personal projects

- Importing startup/business framing (adoption, ROI, market, success metrics) — there is no market.
- Over-engineering for a single user; building infrastructure before the thing works once.
- Treating "will you keep using it" as guaranteed — it's the #1 risk.
- Planning a long roadmap when a weekend version would prove it.
